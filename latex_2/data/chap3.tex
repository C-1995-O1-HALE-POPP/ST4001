\chapter{Discussion and Conclusion}

\section{Summary of Findings}

The experimental in Chapter~2 showed a consistent pattern: 
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Long-horizon clinical reasoning remains challenging, especially for Next Action Prediction;
    \item Discharge Decision is easier and should not be used as a proxy for the entire reasoning benchmark;
    \item Event Memory can modestly improve Argument Prediction but does not reliably improve average decision-making performance;
    \item Historical visit injection, RAG, and Mem0 do not provide systematic gains over the immediate-context baseline; and
    \item DeepSeek's model-native thinking mode can degrade performance through increased conservatism.
\end{enumerate}

These findings collectively reveal a retrieval-reasoning gap: a disconnect between LLMs' ability to locate relevant historical information and their ability to integrate that information into proactive clinical planning. RAG and long-term memory systems optimize access to relevant context \cite{lewis2020rag,mem0,amem2025,amabench2026}, but for clinical decision-making, retrieval is not always the bottleneck. The model can access relevant history, either in full context or through retrieval, yet still fail to use that history to choose a better action.

\section{The Retrieval-Reasoning Gap}


This negative result is therefore informative. It suggests that the limitation is not only the amount of available context, but the absence of mechanisms that connect retrieved evidence to the current decision. The relevant target for future work is not simply retrieving more history, but modeling why a prior event matters, whether it changed the patient's trajectory, and how it should affect the next clinical action. The qualitative traces in Appendix~A.5 illustrate these failure modes at the individual-query level.

The contrast with EHR-Bench \cite{2025_ehr_r1} and recent temporal EHR reasoning work \cite{2025_timer,qu2026tracetemporalreasoningagentic} further clarifies the gap. These studies show that LLMs can model clinical trajectories with non-trivial accuracy, but the agent-based setting studied here shows that this ability does not automatically transfer to interactive decision-making, where history must be selectively retrieved, judged for relevance, and converted into a plan. Static next-event prediction and interactive planning are therefore complementary but distinct capabilities. We hypothesize three contributing mechanisms:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Clinical reasoning requires causal, not merely associative, inference: determining whether a prior treatment was effective requires understanding why it was given and how the patient responded.
    \item Longitudinal EHR data have a low signal-to-noise ratio, so a small number of decision-relevant events may be buried in hundreds of routine records.
    \item Current LLM training data lack the temporal-causal structure of clinical trajectories, creating a distribution shift that retrieval alone cannot remove.
\end{itemize}

\section{Benchmark Validity and Clinical Ground Truth}

\subsection{LongMedBench}
The broader LongMedBench design includes separate retrieval, temporal reconstruction, and reasoning for clinical action. Factual QA primarily tests whether a model can find information in long clinical histories, Temporal Reasoning tests event and visit ordering, and the Long-Horizon Reasoning Task studied here asks whether the model can turn longitudinal evidence into an explicit rationale and then into an action or decision. This separation aligns with emerging virtual EHR and clinical-agent benchmarks that evaluate agent behavior rather than isolated medical knowledge alone \cite{jiang2025medagentbench,liao2026agentehr}. The dissociation across these task families sharpens the central claim: memory augmentation may help retrieval-oriented tasks without reliably improving decision-making.

\subsection{Clinical Ground Truth}
LongMedBench derives labels from real EHR trajectories, but an observed clinical record is not automatically a perfect ground truth. A subsequent order, medication, or discharge timing reflects the actual care process under local constraints, documentation habits, clinician preferences, resource availability, and possibly clinical error. This limitation is central to clinical decision-making evaluation, where real-world action may not equal to gold answer \cite{hager2024evaluation}. The decision task should therefore be understood as evaluation on whether an agent can identify the action most consistent with the observed patient trajectory, not whether it can certify the globally optimal clinical treatment. In our experiments, several design choices mitigate, but do not eliminate, this limitation:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The multiple-choice format uses clinically plausible alternatives, turning evaluation into a context-discrimination problem rather than open-ended  generation.
    \item The 24-hour time-decay score described in Chapter~2 also rewards temporally proximate matching events instead of exact agreement with a single timestamp.
\end{itemize}

These mechanisms acknowledge that multiple clinically reasonable actions may exist at a given state. Nevertheless, they cannot replace prospective expert adjudication; human clinician review remains necessary to distinguish truly wrong answers from acceptable alternatives.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}

LongMedBench is built from MIMIC-IV, a single-institution database, and the filtered cohort emphasizes high-utilization patients with matched discharge notes. Its event representation reduces noise by focusing on abnormal laboratory results and salient structured events, but this may underrepresent the difficulty of distinguishing normal variation from pathology. Cross-institutional validation on datasets such as eICU \cite{pollard2018eicu}, HiRID \cite{2021_hirid_icu}, or STARR \cite{callahan2023stanford} is therefore needed. Future work should also include dedicated evaluation of medically adapted language models, rather than testing only general-purpose LLMs, to determine whether domain-specific pretraining or clinical instruction tuning improves longitudinal reasoning. 

The decision tasks also simplify real clinical practice into automatically templated Next Action Prediction, Argument Prediction, and Discharge Decision queries. This improves diagnostic clarity and scalable construction, but omits factors such as dosage, contraindications, patient preferences, resources, and guideline compliance; Discharge Decision is also weakly discriminative in the sampled evaluation. Future work should add clinician adjudication, adversarial or counterfactual cases, stronger memory architectures for temporal evidence, multimodal data, and outcome-linked evaluation such as mortality, readmission, or length of stay.

\section{Reproducibility}

MIMIC-IV requires credentialed access through PhysioNet, but to support reproducibility and future extensions, we will release the data processing pipeline, template-based task generation logic, and evaluation harness if LongMedBench is admitted by MICCAI 2026. API-based model evaluation used default settings and fixed random seeds where applicable, and the released codebase documents full prompt templates, memory formatting conventions, and decision queries for replication.

\section{Conclusion}

Clinical practice is inherently longitudinal, requiring clinicians to integrate evidence accumulated across years of patient history. This thesis introduced the Long-Horizon Reasoning Task of LongMedBench to evaluate whether contemporary LLM agents can use such historical information through a three-tier memory architecture and systematic ablation experiments.

The central finding is that memory augmentation does not significantly improve longitudinal clinical decision-making: accuracy remains dominated by immediate context, while historical information adds only marginal value. The bottleneck is therefore not just retrieval, but integration. Closing this retrieval-reasoning gap will require architectures that reason over temporal clinical evidence and benchmarks that distinguish empirical EHR trajectories from clinically optimal decisions.
